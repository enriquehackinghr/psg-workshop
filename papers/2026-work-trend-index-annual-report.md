## Foreword

*by Dr. Karim Lakhani, Dorothy & Michael Hintze Professor of Business Administration at Harvard Business School. He specializes in technology management, innovation, digital transformation and AI, and is founder and co-director of the Laboratory for Innovation Science at Harvard, principal investigator of the NASA Tournament Laboratory, and cofounder and chair of the Harvard Business School AI Institute.*

Every era of business is defined by a dominant managerial question. The industrial era asked how to scale production. The information age asked how to digitize and coordinate the enterprise. The emerging AI era asks something more fundamental: how should work itself be designed when intelligence can be embedded, distributed, and increasingly delegated?

The report argues that the most consequential change underway is not simply the adoption of new tools. It is the emergence of a new operating model. That distinction matters. Business models describe how firms create and capture value, but operating models determine how that value is actually delivered, through workflows, roles, decision rights, governance, and the everyday architecture of execution. When the operating model changes, management changes with it.

That is why the rise of AI agents should be understood as more than the next wave of software. As AI moves from assisting with isolated tasks to participating in workflows across functions and systems, leaders must rethink the fundamental design of the enterprise. Work is no longer organized only around people, processes, and applications. Increasingly, it is organized across people, agents, and the systems that connect them. The central task of leadership is shifting from deploying technology to leading and enabling teams to redesign work and processes.

The most forward-looking organizations are beginning to see that AI does not merely automate execution; it changes the location of human value. As execution becomes more scalable, the premium on judgment rises. As expertise becomes more abundant, the ability to orchestrate it becomes more important. As experimentation becomes easier, organizations must become better at learning. The firms that will benefit most from AI are unlikely to be those that simply accumulate the largest number of tools or pilots. They will be the ones that build operating models capable of turning local gains into institutional advantage.

This report focuses attention on three interdependent levels of change: the employee, the leader, and the organization. At the employee level, AI expands what individuals can accomplish and changes the boundary between execution and higher-order work. At the leadership level, it raises new questions about delegation, accountability, escalation, and trust. At the organizational level, it makes learning itself a source of advantage, because the firms that capture, codify, and diffuse what they learn will improve faster than those that leave insight trapped in local experiments.

Leaders should resist the temptation to treat this transition as frictionless or inevitable. The path from adoption to advantage is neither linear nor automatic. AI can extend capability, but it can also create overconfidence. Agents can accelerate work, but they can also expose brittle processes, unclear decision rights, and weak governance. Productivity gains at the edge do not automatically become enterprise transformation at the core. The challenge ahead is not simply technical. It is managerial, organizational, and strategic.

The question is no longer whether AI matters. It is whether the firm is willing to redesign itself around what AI now makes possible. That means rethinking how work is divided, where judgment resides, how expertise is codified, how incentives reinforce reinvention, and how governance keeps pace with increasingly agentic systems. The organizations that learn fastest, not just those that deploy fastest, will be best positioned to lead.

If earlier eras of management were defined by the design of scale, this one will be defined by the design of judgment, learning, and coordinated action across humans and machines.

## Introduction

The opportunity for human potential at work has never been greater. People are using AI and agents to expand what they can do and who gets to do it. Call it the new agency equation: as agents take on more of the execution, humans increasingly have more agency, more room to direct the work, make the calls, and own the outcomes. For every firm, the imperative now is to turn that agency into unprecedented value.

The research analyzed trillions of anonymized Microsoft 365 productivity signals and surveyed 20,000 workers using AI across 10 countries, supplemented by interviews with leading experts in AI, work, and organizational psychology. The anxiety around AI at work is real, from fears of job loss to the pressure to keep up. But the research shows a growing share of workers are using AI in advanced, resourceful ways. The problem is that most organizations are not keeping up.

In many cases, people are ready. The systems around them are not. The constraint for most firms is the gap between what their employees can now do and what their organizations are built to support. Organizational factors such as culture, manager support, and talent practices account for twice the reported AI impact of individual effort alone.

Bridging that gap means redesigning the operating model across employees, leaders, and the organization. The firms already doing it, called Frontier Firms, are pulling ahead fast. Employees are using AI to lift the ceiling on what they can do. Leaders are rearchitecting work itself, deciding what humans and AI do. And organizations are turning into Learning Systems, because the companies that learn fastest from their own work will win.

> **AI users** are survey respondents who reported using generative AI for work at least occasionally. Those who selected "never" or "don't know" were screened out.
>
> **AI impact** is a combination of outcome variables about how AI users report AI is making an impact, including being more creative, doing new kinds of work, giving higher-quality first drafts, improving work ability, collaboration, feeling in control, improving career prospects, doing high-value work, and being more likely to stay at the company because of AI.

## Employees: AI lifts the ceiling on individual potential

AI is expanding what we can do, and putting a premium on judgment, clarity of intent, and the design of work itself. A privacy-preserving analysis of more than 100,000 chats in Microsoft 365 Copilot shows that 49% of all conversations support cognitive work, helping workers analyze information, solve problems, evaluate, and think creatively. The remainder splits among working with people (19%), producing work (17%), and finding information (15%).

Employees at every level now have a partner that helps them analyze, synthesize, and deepen their own expertise, while also building expertise in other areas. AI is not just helping us do things faster. It is expanding who can do high-value work.

### The data

- 66% of AI users say AI has allowed them to spend more time on high-value work.
- 58% say they are producing work they could not have a year ago. That rises to 80% among Frontier Professionals.
- Frontier Professionals use agents for multi-step workflows and building multi-agent systems, routinely rethink workflows, and create shared AI standards for their team or organization. They are 16% of the AI users surveyed, a small but disproportionately valuable group.

As AI expands what people can do, it also raises the premium on good judgment. Asked which human skills are more important as AI takes on more work, AI users named two at the top: quality control of AI output (50%) and critical thinking (46%). And 86% say they treat AI output as a starting point, not a final answer, and that they stay responsible for the thinking.

Frontier Professionals are even more aware of the importance of human judgment. They rank higher across every measure related to critical thinking and quality control. They are more likely than non-Frontier Professionals to intentionally do some work without AI to keep their skills sharp (43% vs. 30%) and to intentionally pause before starting work to decide what should be done by AI versus a human (53% vs. 33%). They refuse to outsource their thinking.

### From expertise to agency

As AI use matures, the most effective AI users will not be the ones who do more things faster. They will be the ones who redefine their value around what only humans can do: setting clear intent (defining the desired outcome and quality bar) and designing how the work gets done across humans and AI. They apply judgment and taste, build trust, and shape systems that produce better outcomes. The question stops being "What tasks define my job?" and starts being "What outcomes am I now positioned to drive?"

### Beyond the prompt: the four modes of working with AI

How people work with AI depends on two independent dimensions: how the human engages with the work (human intensity, ranging from directing to supervising) and how much the agent is used (agent intensity, ranging from assistant to teammate). Four modes result.

| Mode | Description | Looks like |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Delegation** | The human sets direction; the agent executes | Turning raw notes into a structured recap or draft; packaging a recurring report from standard inputs; compiling a source-grounded research summary once scope is defined |
| **Collaboration** | The work needs both human and agent | Refining a proposal through multiple rounds of feedback; building an analysis where each result reshapes the next question; drafting a communication where tone and framing require judgment |
| **Asking** | Quick asks that call for quick exchanges | Looking up a fact, date, or definition; rewriting a sentence for clarity or tone; reformatting a small table or converting units |
| **Exploration** | Testing what AI can do | Testing whether the tool can handle a new workflow before relying on it; trying different prompt strategies; probing the edges of what an agent can do autonomously |

What sets Frontier Professionals apart is not which mode they use; it is knowing which mode a task calls for. Routine execution, research, and synthesis get delegated. As AI does more of the work, humans stay involved by setting direction and taking responsibility for how outputs are used.

### Perspectives: what does "human" work look like now?

- **Aneesh Raman, Chief Economic Opportunity Officer, LinkedIn:** The human brain remains the most complex object in the known universe and has existed far longer than the technology of the industrial age. We are going to return to the fundamentals that make us human.
- **Dr. Laura Hamill, Director of Research, AI@Work Thought Leadership, Microsoft:** What is exciting is starting to think about how to solve problems we never thought we could solve before.
- **Conor Grennan, CEO and Founder, AI Mindset:** The future of human work lies in judging quality of output, figuring out directions to point AI, and bringing people along.

## Leaders: The job of every leader is to rearchitect work

Most organizations are not yet built to capture the value of this expanded human agency. The challenge is not isolated to tools or individuals. It is a breakdown across the system that connects leadership, culture, management practices, and how work is measured.

To understand where the breakdown occurs, the research mapped survey respondents across two dimensions: individual capability with AI, and organizational readiness to absorb it. Individual capability reflects how broadly respondents use AI, how confidently they direct and judge it, how actively they experiment and share learnings, and whether they report creating new value. Organizational readiness reflects the surrounding environment: culture and management practices that support AI use, clear rules for how people and AI work together, and whether AI use is encouraged and recognized.

### The Transformation Paradox: workers are ready, organizations are not

The mapping reveals five groups of AI users.

| Zone | Share | Definition |
|------|-------|-----------|
| **Frontier** | 19% | High individual capability and high organizational readiness, reinforcing each other |
| **Emergent** | 50% | Both individual practice and organizational conditions are still taking shape |
| **Stalled** | 16% | Both individual capability and organizational support are low |
| **Blocked agency** | 10% | High individual capability, low organizational support |
| **Unclaimed capacity** | 5% | Low individual capability, high organizational readiness |

In many cases, employees are moving faster than the organizations around them. About 31% of AI users are misaligned (blocked agency plus unclaimed capacity), and the largest share sits in the messy middle.

This misalignment is reinforced at the top. Only one in four AI users (26%) say their leadership is clearly and consistently aligned on AI. Leaders are also more likely than employees to say AI-driven reinvention feels safe and rewarded.

A pressure point emerges where the pull to perform collides with the push to transform: 65% of AI users fear falling behind if they do not adapt quickly with AI, yet 45% say it feels safer to focus on current goals than to redesign work. Only 13% say they are rewarded for reinvention even when results are not immediately met.

This is the Transformation Paradox: employees are ready to reinvent how they work, but the surrounding system of metrics, incentives, and norms continues to reinforce the old way. The same forces accelerating AI adoption are holding it back.

> Leaders were more likely than employees to report feeling safe suggesting new ways of working with AI (81% vs. 67%) and that their managers create space for experimentation (78% vs. 59%). They were 2x more likely to report that reinvention is rewarded regardless of outcome (21% vs. 10%).

### Leadership must redesign the system to match the work

The job of every leader right now is to make change stick. That means setting strategy at the top and ensuring metrics, incentives, and expectations reward people for changing how they work. Once strategy is set, managers operationalize it.

A separate study of 1,800 workers globally found that when managers actively modeled AI use, employees reported a 17-point lift in reported AI value, a 22-point lift in critical thinking about their AI use, and a 30-point lift in trust in agentic AI. When managers created psychological safety around experimentation, employees reported up to 20 points higher AI readiness and value, and were 1.4x more likely to be high-frequency users of agentic AI.

Frontier Professionals consistently work in this kind of environment. Compared to non-Frontier Professionals, they are more likely to say their manager openly uses AI (85% vs. 64%), sets quality standards for AI work (83% vs. 57%), creates space for experimentation (84% vs. 61%), and encourages more ambitious work redesign (87% vs. 61%). They are 2x more likely to say they are rewarded for reinvention regardless of outcome (26% vs. 11%).

The Transformation Paradox is, at its core, a systems problem. And systems do not fix themselves. They have to be redesigned.

### Perspectives: what can leaders do to catch up with their own employees?

- **Conor Grennan, CEO and Founder, AI Mindset:** If leaders are totally bought in, especially top leadership, it changes everything.
- **Dr. Karim Lakhani, Professor of Business Administration, Harvard Business School:** You have to invest a great deal of time and effort to map the dependencies between people and processes, then ask where you can make a difference.
- **Dr. Laura Hamill, Director of Research, AI@Work Thought Leadership, Microsoft:** Leaders actually need to use AI, play with it, and try to build new things with it. Otherwise they will not understand what is being discussed.

## Organizations: Every firm is a Learning System

The firms pulling ahead are focused on AI absorption rather than just AI adoption, redesigning how work gets done and turning output into insight. When that insight is captured, shared, and built into how the organization operates, it creates a self-reinforcing Learning System.

Many leaders focus on hiring the right people and assume results will follow. The data shows it is something else: the conditions leaders create for that talent to thrive.

### AI value: the biggest factor behind AI impact is organizational, not individual

Testing a broad set of organizational, individual, and demographic factors against self-reported AI impact, organizational factors (culture, manager support, talent practices) account for more than 2x the reported AI impact of individual factors like mindset and behavior (67% vs. 32%).

The three strongest correlates are all organizational:

| Factor | Strength relative to top factor |
|--------|--------------------------------|
| Org AI culture | 100% |
| Talent practices | 43% |
| Manager support | 43% |
| AI mindset (top individual factor) | 42% |

The strongest single factor, the organization's AI culture, is about two and a half times as strong a signal as the top individual factor. The findings underscore the importance of an AI-ready environment: a culture that treats AI as a strategic advantage and encourages experimentation, managers who model and incentivize AI use, and talent practices that build skills and create space to apply them.

The real question is not whether people have the right skills. It is whether the organization is built to unlock them.

### Redesigning systems and processes

The number of active agents in the Microsoft 365 ecosystem grew 15x year over year, rising to 18x in large enterprises. As agents take on more, they generate valuable signals: what worked, what failed, where outcomes drifted. In many organizations, those signals stay local or spread slowly. Frontier Firms capture these signals and encode them into shared routines, improving future work while preserving accountability and control.

Frontier Professionals are more likely than non-Frontier Professionals to say their teams:

- Brainstorm and refine business processes together to identify AI opportunities (63% vs. 32%)
- Share AI tips, new agents, learnings, and mistakes (61% vs. 36%)
- Discuss quality standards for AI-assisted work (54% vs. 29%)

They are also more likely to report that agent workflows, human handoffs, and quality standards are documented and repeatable at the team (26% vs. 19%), function (29% vs. 17%), and organization level (25% vs. 14%).

### State of agents: breadth in some industries, depth in others

Agents are now used in every industry, but adoption patterns vary widely. In software and technology, adoption is broad, accounting for nearly one in five of all firms using agents. Others, like manufacturing, account for a smaller share of companies using agents but deploy them at much greater scale within each organization. Individual behavior stays consistent across industries; the real difference lies in where agents are embedded and how extensively organizations integrate them into workflows.

### Building an evaluation infrastructure

Creating these systems requires a disciplined approach to holding humans accountable for the work agents execute. The more agents execute, the higher the stakes around human evaluation. Approving one bad output is manageable, but when bad outputs make it through at scale, the risk compounds. The key is an evaluation infrastructure that can keep up with agents.

It starts with three questions every Frontier Firm needs to answer:

1. Who reviews agent performance?
2. Who has the authority to update the workflows that agents run?
3. How does a local win get captured and scaled across the organization?

Organizations that can answer these questions are building Owned Intelligence: institutional know-how that compounds over time, is unique to the firm, and is hard to replicate.

Building that infrastructure requires coordinated reinvention across four roles:

- **Employees** rearchitect their work around intent and review.
- **Leaders** redesign processes around outcomes and agent autonomy.
- **IT** builds the infrastructure for agent operations at scale, treating agents as managed entities with identities, permissions, policy enforcement, and lifecycle management. IT becomes the control plane for agent operations.
- **Security** ensures trust is woven into the system, accounting for new risks such as data exfiltration, unintended system actions, and unauthorized access by embedding monitoring, policy enforcement, and auditability directly into the platform.

When these four roles work in concert, the organization becomes a Learning System: one in which work continuously produces insight, and insight continuously reshapes how work gets done.

You are building systems that keep changing because the AI systems keep changing.

## Looking ahead

The firms that build a new operating model today will not just move faster in the short term. They will build something more durable: an organization that learns faster than its competitors, compounds its own intelligence, and gets harder to catch with every cycle.

This shift will not happen easily. Some jobs will change, some will go away, and many that do not exist yet will emerge. According to LinkedIn's 2026 Labor Market Report, in the past two years employers have created at least 1.3 million AI-related job opportunities, including data annotators, AI engineers, and forward-deployed engineers. These roles did not exist five years ago but have quickly become essential to digital economies. This dynamism is not new to work, but its pace and scale are, and the uncertainty people feel is real.

What is also real: the potential for employees to make an impact has never been higher. Leaders are starting to redesign the systems around them. The organizations capturing what their work is teaching them are learning faster than the ones that are not. None of that happens by accident.

The opportunity in front of every leader and organization is to take control: to build a place where agents amplify what people can do, where human judgment stays at the center of the work that matters, and where everyone has the agency to decide what comes next.

## Methodology

### Microsoft 365 telemetry

All telemetry data is based on aggregated and anonymized Microsoft 365 productivity signals.

- **Prompts per user:** Number of prompts per user in a rolling 28-day period. Includes all countries and customer segments (including EU), excludes government. Data spans March 2025 through March 2026.
- **Share of user goals by O\*NET generalized work activities:** The percentage of total Intermediate Work Activities (IWAs) detected in Copilot conversations that map to Generalized Work Activities (GWA), using a fractional share method. Commercial segments excluding education, North American countries only, 105,000 samples from one week of February 2026.
- **Monthly active agents growth:** Year-over-year change in the count of unique active agents across the Microsoft 365 Copilot Agents platform and SharePoint in a rolling 28-day period. An agent counts as active with at least one day of user-initiated usage or one autonomous run in the period. Data spans March 2025 through March 2026.
- **Monthly active firms:** Count of unique firms with at least one user who made at least one intentional agent usage in a rolling 28-day period.

### Study: Empowering managers for an AI-first future

The Microsoft People Science team analyzed survey responses from 1,800 employees globally as part of the Microsoft People Science Agentic Teaming & Trust Survey (July 2025), including 819 leaders (C-suite, VP, Director), 520 managers, and 461 individual contributors.

### Work Trend Index Survey

Conducted by Edelman Data x Intelligence among 20,000 full-time employed or self-employed knowledge workers who use AI at work across 10 markets, between February 18 and April 7, 2026. The survey was 20 minutes, online, in English or local language. 2,000 workers were surveyed per market. Markets: Australia, Brazil, France, Germany, India, Italy, Japan, Netherlands, United Kingdom, and United States.

**Audience definitions:**

- **AI users:** Knowledge workers who reported using generative AI for work at least occasionally. "Never" or "don't know" respondents were screened out.
- **Employees:** Knowledge workers not in middle to upper job levels and without influence on decision-making.
- **Leaders:** Knowledge workers in middle to upper job levels (Senior Director through C-Suite) with at least some decision-making influence.

### Organization vs. Employee AI Readiness Index

Places each respondent on two self-reported dimensions: individual readiness (a composite of usage sophistication, self-efficacy, proactive behaviors, and self-reported value creation) and organizational readiness (a composite of organizational AI culture, manager support, governance maturity, and AI in performance evaluation). Both dimensions are normalized 0 to 1 within each market before pooling. Each respondent is assigned to one of five mutually exclusive zones: Frontier (19%), Blocked Agency (10%), Unclaimed Capacity (5%), Stalled (16%), and Emergent (50%). The index is calculated on 16,971 respondents. Pearson correlation between the two dimensions is r = 0.55.

### Frontier Professionals

Classified only if they reported a combination of three distinct behavior sets: advanced use of AI agents for complex or multi-step work; routine redesign of workflows to take advantage of AI; and participation in structured, repeatable AI-enabled practices that scale beyond individual use. They represent 3,233 of the 20,000 AI users surveyed. 44% are business decision makers, 56% employees. By generation: 50% millennials, 23% Gen X, 22% Gen Z, 4% Boomers. They are more likely to work in tech (35%) or financial services (12%), in IT (36%) or finance and accounting (11%) roles, and in larger organizations (53% in companies with 500+ employees).

### AI Impact Analysis

Identifies which of 29 measured factors are most strongly associated with workers reporting real AI impact. The factors are organized into three categories: organizational environment (10 factors), individual mindset and behavior (9 factors), and demographics (10 factors). Importance is measured by random forest permutation, with cross-validation by elastic net regression and gradient-boosted trees. All three model families produce the same category ranking and top correlates, with held-out test R² of 0.680, 0.689, and 0.690 respectively. Run on 19,854 respondents after removing missing values. Every variable is self-reported by the same respondent at the same moment, so relationships shown are statistical associations, not causal effects.

**Composite factor definitions:**

- **AI Impact:** Outcome variables on how employees report AI is making an impact, including being more creative, doing new kinds of work, higher-quality first drafts, improved work ability, collaboration, feeling in control, career prospects, high-value work, and likelihood to stay at the company.
- **Org AI culture:** How strongly the organization's culture aligns with AI (openness and curiosity, safety to suggest new ways of working, confidence in ability to use AI). Built from 10 survey items.
- **Manager support:** How much the direct manager actively supports AI use (encouraging experiments, modeling AI use, making room for AI-enabled work in evaluation, making it safe to try new things). Built from 5 items.
- **Talent practices:** How much AI is built into talent practices (investing in skills, encouraging new domains or projects, professional development support). Built from 6 items.
- **AI mindset:** The respondent's own attitude toward AI (confidence, openness, trust). Built from 10 items.

### Core 10 Market Data: key global trends

- 16% of total workers are Frontier Professionals; the remaining 84% are not.
- 58% of AI users say they are producing work they could not have a year ago (80% among Frontier Professionals).
- Top human skills cited as more important as AI takes on more work: quality control of AI output (50%) and critical thinking (46%).

- 65% of AI users fear falling behind if they do not adapt with AI quickly, yet 45% say it feels safer to focus on current goals than to redesign work.
